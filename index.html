Recession probability model

A machine-learning model runs in your browser to estimate how likely a US recession is over the next 12 months, using the same macro relationships shown in charts 1, 2, and 4.

  • Inputs: 60 months of rolling correlations (GDP vs S&P 500, GDP vs jobs, yield curve vs GDP), their recent changes and volatility, and the yield-curve spread.
  • Method: A neural network trained on decades of monthly data outputs month-by-month hazard scores, which are combined into one probability and calibrated against NBER recession dates.
  • On the charts: Hover any date to see what the model would have forecast from that month — correlation paths plus a recession window when hazard is elevated. Dashed lines and bands on the latest view extend the correlation forecast forward.

Probabilities answer a binary question — “Does a recession start within 12 months?” — so calibrated scores often sit near 0% or high values when signals are clear. Intermediate percentages (for example 15–55%) appear in ambiguous transition periods, especially for the current outlook rather than years before a recession that is already known in hindsight.

Sources: Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED), National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER), Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), Cleveland Fed Inflation Nowcast.
